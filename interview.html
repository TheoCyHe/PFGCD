An Interview with Jayron Mardani on Handling the Issue of Gentrification-Linked Displacement

Photo Source: Columbia Daily Spectator

Jayron Mardani is a BA Candidate in Urban Studies at Columbia University, and has been involved with Columbia's Housing Equity Project. This interview was conducted by Theodore He on 06/13/21.



1. What are your thoughts on the link between gentrification/displacement and existing social inequalities?

By definition, the term stems from the concept of the “gentry”, or people of good social standing, moving into a relatively disinvested neighborhood, initiating a series of upgrades and transformation that result in the displacement of the original residents of the neighborhood. In essence, it is the replacement of the existing population by the gentry. This dynamic of gentry versus original population fundamentally feeds on existing social inequalities. A prime example seen in the United States are the lasting harmful effects of racial segregation and discrimination. Through state-sanctioned practices such as redlining and discriminatory lending, neighborhoods of color often suffer from massive disinvestment. These factors among many more often result in low property values- something that can attract pioneer gentrifiers. As more and more gentrifiers take advantage of low property values (sometimes the added appeal of moving into an “authentic neighborhood”), markets respond to these attractions. Slowly, the shifting demographics of the neighborhood result in elements meant to cater to the incoming gentry rather than the original residents, and as demand increases, property values rise as well. All these processes work to alienate the original residents and exploit the environment that was created by discriminatory practices.


2. What do you think are the best solutions to gentrification-caused displacement? What is your opinion on the idea of gentrifying without displacing (e.g. by reducing property tax for long-time residents, expanding public housing, or establishing community benefit agreements with investors) – is it entirely unrealistic, or something to aim for?

Working to prevent physical displacement is definitely something to aim for. The suggestion of establishing a CBA with investors is one that can produce mixed results, and sometimes convolute the immediate needs of long-term residents. Increasing access to subsidized housing is another straightforward-sounding solution that of course requires larger commitment from the developers, managers, and neighborhood residents. The construction of subsidized housing is often contested by the gentry as an undesirable addition to a neighborhood, which may be negatively reflected in the markets, meaning that there are various aspects and ramifications of such a development that need to considered and controlled for by all parties involved. All of this being said, physical displacement is not the only form of displacement that long-time residents are subject to. While a long-term resident may be able to remain in their neighborhood. the rapidly changing dynamics and demographics of their neighborhood unquestionably create a sense of isolation and displacement. The neighborhood as they knew it- the community, familiarity, security, has changed.


3. Even if low-income residents are able to stay in a gentrified neighborhood, the character of the neighborhood often shifts completely to cater to new, more well-off residents, alienating those that remain. How can this effect be prevented or mitigated?

In my opinion, this seems difficult to fully achieve. I imagine that it requires a deep level of understanding from incoming residents occupying the neighborhood. The people that move in to gentrifying neighborhoods bear a large responsibility. It’s important to acknowledge the role one is playing in the larger systems of displacement and inequality. I’m hesitant to say that cultural displacement can be fully prevented, but mitigation is something to strive for (even if it may seem unattainable). Maintaining the authentic culture of a neighborhood as it undergoes gentrification is often something that is fought for by long-term residents, and is sometimes overlooked by the larger parties involved such as development corporations, wealthy institutions, or incoming residents. Temporary fixes may include restrictions on large corporations or chain businesses moving into a neighborhood, but the idealism of such solutions must be met face-to- face with the often compelling monetary incentives given to such corporations.


4. Who should hold the responsibility of preventing displacement? The government? Urban development projects? What about the responsibility of helping already-displaced people?

The systems that perpetuate the harms of gentrification and displacement are extremely complex in that they entwine entities as large as the federal government to those as insignificant as a single person searching for an apartment. At risk of oversimplification, the government holds an extremely large responsibility in preventing and mitigating displacement. Throughout US history, federal, state, and local governments have allowed and sometimes even created methods of active discrimination and neglect against disadvantaged populations. Even with recent acknowledgements of the harms of the past, some institutions are hesitant to acknowledge the lasting effects that continue to ravage said populations. There needs to be a complete understanding of the state’s (continued) role in the historical practices and ideologies that have resulted in the societal crises we face today. Only then can there be comprehensive proposals on what must be done to support the communities that have been left behind for so long. These proposals may include actions such as increased funding and investment directly into disadvantaged neighborhoods, altered zoning laws and tax incentives, and much more (not excluding reparations).
